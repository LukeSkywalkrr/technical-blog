Engineering, told slowly

How we build
the feed that never
ends

You've scrolled one today. Maybe for an hour. It felt like nothing — content just kept arriving. That "nothing" is one of the hardest things to build in software. Let's build it together, one problem at a time.

◆ A build-along ◆ ~15 min read ◆ No prior magic required
01 The trick you never noticed

It's supposed to feel like nothing

Here's the strange thing about infinite scroll: when it works perfectly, you don't notice it at all. You flick your thumb, more posts appear, and you keep going. There's no "Next Page" button. No loading spinner that makes you wait. It just flows.

That seamlessness is the whole product. The second a user feels a stutter — a blank gap, a jump, a wait — the spell breaks and they close the app. So the goal we're chasing isn't "show more posts." It's much sneakier than that:

Make an endless river of content feel effortless, while a machine somewhere sweats to keep it flowing.

To pull this off, the app has to quietly ask a server for the next batch of posts before you even reach the bottom. That server has to figure out which posts you specifically want to see, put them in the right order, and hand them back — all in under half a second. Every time. For hundreds of millions of people at once.

Quick word A server is just another computer, sitting in a data center, whose whole job is to answer requests from your phone. When we say "the backend," we mean the servers and databases doing the work you never see.

So let's start where every engineer starts: with the obvious, simple idea. And then let's watch it fall apart. Because understanding why the simple idea breaks is the entire key to building the real thing.

◆ Warmed up. Now the first real problem →
02 The obvious idea

"Just grab the next twenty"

Imagine every post lives in a giant list in the database, newest at the top. When you open the app, we hand you the first 20. You scroll to the bottom, and we say to the database: "skip the 20 you already sent, give me the next 20." Scroll again: "skip 40, give me 20."

This has a name. It's called offset pagination, and it's the first thing almost everyone reaches for. In database language it looks like this:

SELECT * FROM posts
ORDER BY created_at DESC
LIMIT 20 OFFSET 100000 -- skip 100,000, then give me 20
Quick word A database is an organized warehouse for data — think of a spreadsheet with billions of rows. OFFSET means "skip this many rows first." LIMIT means "then give me this many."

It's clean. It's easy to understand. You could even build old-school numbered pages with it — jump straight to page 50. For a small app, it works fine.

And then your app gets popular. And it quietly starts to die.

03 Crack #1 — it gets slower the deeper you go

The database can't teleport

Here's the part that surprises people. When you say "skip 100,000 rows," the database can't magically leap to row 100,001. It has no teleporter. It has to walk past all 100,000 rows, one by one, counting them off, throwing every single one away — and only then start collecting the 20 you asked for.

The problem

The further a user scrolls, the more rows the database has to count and discard before it reaches the good part. Scroll deep enough and a single request that should take milliseconds takes seconds — long enough that the request times out and the user sees a broken feed.

This isn't a small tax. Watch what happens to the response time as a real user scrolls deeper into a table of a million posts. The bars below tell the whole story — offset in warm red, and (spoiler) the fix we're building toward in teal.

By page 5,000, offset is over 600 times slower than it needs to be. And notice the teal bar at the bottom: whatever page you're on, it barely moves. That flatness is the target. Hold onto it — we'll earn it in a minute.

04 Crack #2 — the feed shifts under your feet

The ghost of the duplicate post

Speed isn't even the worst of it. There's a second crack, and it's the kind that makes users think your app is broken.

Picture this. You've just read posts 1 through 20. While you were reading, five new posts got published at the top of the feed — totally normal, this happens thousands of times a second on a busy app. Now you scroll down and the app asks for "the next 20, skip the first 20."

But the list has shifted. Those five new posts pushed everything down by five slots. So "skip 20" now lands you five posts too early — and you get shown five posts you already read.

The problem

Because offset counts by position, and positions move whenever content is added or removed, users see duplicate posts when things are inserted, and silently skip posts when things are deleted. On a live feed, the ground is always moving.

So now we have two separate wounds from the same idea: it's slow, and it's unreliable. Both come from one root cause — and naming that root cause is the moment everything clicks.

◆ Two problems diagnosed. Now — how do we think about the fix?
05 The shift in thinking

Stop counting positions. Start dropping a bookmark.

Here's the insight that fixes both problems at once. Every pain we just felt came from one habit: we described where to continue by counting from the start. "Skip 100,000." "Skip 20." Counting is fragile because the thing you're counting keeps changing.

So flip it. Instead of remembering how many posts you've seen, remember the last exact post you saw. Not "I'm 20 posts in" but "the last thing I read was that specific post, published at 12:00:00, with ID 456. Just give me what comes after it."

It's the difference between telling a friend "start reading on page 213" versus "start right after the sentence about the lighthouse." If someone adds or removes a paragraph, the page number is wrong — but "after the lighthouse" is still exactly right. It's an anchor to a real thing, not to a position that drifts.

The name for it This bookmark is called a cursor. The whole approach is cursor-based pagination (engineers also call it keyset pagination). It's what every large-scale feed on earth uses. Everything for the rest of this piece builds on it.

That's the direction. Now let's actually build it and watch both cracks seal shut.

06 Building the real thing

The cursor, step by step

Instead of "skip 20," the app now hands the server the bookmark from the last post it received. The server turns that into a plain, honest question to the database: "give me the posts that come after this exact one."

SELECT * FROM posts
WHERE (created_at, id) < ('2026-04-07T12:00:00Z', 456)
ORDER BY created_at DESC, id DESC
LIMIT 20;

The magic is in that WHERE line. Because the database keeps posts in a sorted index — think of the tabbed dividers in a filing cabinet — it can jump straight to the bookmarked post and start reading from there. No walking past 100,000 rows. No counting. It lands on the spot instantly, no matter how deep you've scrolled.

Quick word An index is a pre-sorted shortcut the database keeps on the side, like the tabbed dividers in a filing cabinet. It's how the database finds one exact record without reading everything before it.

Crack #1, sealed. That flat teal bar from earlier? This is where it comes from. Every page is a direct jump, so every page is fast — page 5 or page 50,000, it doesn't care.

Crack #2, sealed too. New posts appearing at the top can't hurt us anymore, because we never said "skip 20." We said "after post 456." Post 456 is still post 456 no matter how much gets added above it. No duplicates. No skips. The feed is stable.

One sneaky detail: the tiebreaker

Did you notice we bookmarked two things — the timestamp and the ID? That's not an accident. On a busy app, dozens of posts can share the exact same timestamp, right down to the microsecond. If our bookmark only said "after 12:00:00," the database wouldn't know which of those identical-time posts we meant, and could hand back a mess of duplicates or gaps.

So we add the post's unique ID as a tiebreaker. The database checks the time first; if two posts tie, it uses the ID to settle it, precisely. That one extra field is the difference between a feed that's usually right and one that's always right.

We also hide the bookmark

We'd never hand the raw timestamp and ID to the app in plain sight — that would leak how our database is built and let clever people tamper with it. Instead we scramble the bookmark into a short, meaningless-looking token and often sign it (a tamper-proof seal) so nobody can forge one. The app just carries this opaque token around; only our server knows how to read it.

And one clever shortcut: "grab one extra"

How does the app know when to stop — when there's nothing left to load? Counting every remaining post would be expensive. So we cheat, gracefully: if the app wants 20 posts, we quietly ask the database for 21. If 21 come back, we know there's more, so we send the 20 and flip a little "there's more" switch to on. If only 20 or fewer come back, we've hit the end, and we switch it off. One extra row answers the whole question.

O(1)
Roughly constant speed, at any depth
0
Duplicates or skips from a shifting feed
+1
Extra row that tells us when to stop
Quick word O(1) is shorthand engineers use for "the work stays the same no matter how big the data gets." Offset was O(N) — the work grew with the pile. Getting from O(N) to O(1) is the whole ballgame.
◆ The engine works. But the user still sees the screen — let's fix that side too.
07 Keeping the screen calm

The illusion has a second half

A fast, stable backend is only half the promise. The other half lives on your screen. And screens have their own breaking point: they choke when asked to hold too much at once.

If you scrolled for twenty minutes and the app kept every post you passed, your phone would be trying to draw tens of thousands of items at once. The browser grinds, scrolling gets choppy, memory balloons, and eventually the whole thing crashes.

The problem

A phone or browser can't keep thousands of on-screen items alive at once without stuttering and running out of memory. But a "feed that never ends" seems to demand exactly that.

The fix: only draw what fits on the glass

Here's the sleight of hand. At any moment, only about 20 posts actually fit on your screen. So why keep the other 9,980 drawn? We don't. The app draws only the handful you can see (plus a couple just off-screen so nothing flickers), and as you scroll, it recycles those same few slots — pouring new post data into them like water into the same set of glasses.

To keep the scrollbar honest, the app props up an invisible spacer as tall as all the posts would be. So it feels like thousands of items are there — but only a couple dozen ever truly exist. This trick has a name: virtualization, or "windowing," because you're only ever looking through a small moving window onto a huge imaginary list.

Quick word The frontend is the part running on your device — the screen, the buttons, the scrolling. The backend is the servers it talks to. Both have to cooperate for the feed to feel seamless.

The last enemy: the jump

You've felt this one. You go to tap something, an image finishes loading, everything lurches down, and you tap the wrong thing. That lurch is called a layout shift, and it's the fastest way to make an app feel cheap.

The defense is preparation. Before a post's image arrives, the app reserves the exact space it will need — often showing a grey skeleton placeholder in the meantime. And it fetches the next batch early, while you're still a screen or two away from the bottom, so the new posts are already sitting there by the time you arrive. Done right, the seam is invisible. That's the target the big platforms hold themselves to: the whole handoff, start to finish, inside roughly half a second.

08 How the giants really do it

Same idea, planetary muscle

Everything so far is the foundation — and every giant stands on it. But at the scale of hundreds of millions of people, they bolt on extra machinery to decide not just where the feed continues, but what deserves to be in it at all. Here's the flavor of each, in plain terms.

Twitter / X precompute at write time

X figured that people read far more than they post, so it does the heavy lifting the moment someone posts, not when you scroll. When you tweet, a service fans that tweet out and drops its ID straight into the ready-made timelines of your followers — kept in ultra-fast memory so it's waiting the instant they open the app.

The exception is celebrities. Fanning one post out to 100 million followers would jam the system, so posts from the hugely-followed skip the fan-out and get mixed in as you read instead. A blend of "prepared ahead" and "assembled on demand."

Instagram a funnel of rankers

Instagram can't score billions of possible posts for you in half a second, so it narrows the field in stages — a funnel. A cheap, fast model skims the huge pool down to a few thousand maybes; a slower, smarter model studies the finalists and predicts how likely you are to like, click, or hide each one.

It also deliberately fights sameness. If it just chased raw engagement, it would flood you with near-identical posts until you got bored. So it applies a diversity penalty, gently pushing down anything too similar to what it just showed you — keeping the river varied.

LinkedIn recency and self-correction

LinkedIn leans hard on freshness — a system there hard-evicts anything older than 30 days from the main feed index, so stale posts simply can't surface. Its ranking runs in stages too, with a final pass that enforces variety before anything reaches you.

Its cleverest trick handles silence. If a post slides past you and you don't react, that no-reaction is treated as a quiet "no thanks." The system dials that post's score down so the next batch you pull skips it and brings something fresher. The feed literally learns from what you scroll past.

Notice the shared spine under all three. They precompute what they can, assemble the rest on demand, rank hard, and lean on the exact same cursor foundation we built by hand — just wrapped in far more muscle.

09 The whole picture

What "nothing" is actually made of

We started with a feed that felt like nothing to use. Now you know what's underneath: a bookmark instead of a page number, so the database can leap straight to your spot and never trip over a shifting feed. A screen that only ever draws a handful of posts while faking thousands. A frantic little handoff that finishes before you notice it started. And, on top, a ranking brain deciding what's worth your next flick.

The next time content just keeps arriving under your thumb, you'll know the truth — that the effortless part is the hardest part, and a lot of careful engineering is working very hard so that you feel absolutely nothing.
