00 — Prologue

The button that hides a vault.

You tap Pay ₹4,999. A moment later, Payment successful.

Between those two events, several things you did not ask for happened. The shop appeared to receive your card. It did not. The card number appeared to be sent to your bank. It was — but not directly. And nowhere in the shop's own databases is your card actually stored.

Behind that small Pay button is a system solving a stubborn design problem:

  • The shop needs a payment, but the shop should not hold the card.
  • The bank needs the card, but the bank is not on the shop's website.
  • The customer expects a single form and a single button, all inside the shop's page.

Three parties. One page. One moment. Only one of them is allowed to see the sixteen digits.

Let us build the system one problem at a time.

Honesty note

Payment providers do not publish every internal detail of their production systems. What follows is an industry architecture model assembled from public integration behavior, PCI guidance, browser security rules, tokenization standards, and common high-assurance system design — not a claim about any single provider's private implementation.

01 — The illusion

It feels like you gave the shop your card.

You typed the digits into the shop's page. The shop got them. Simple, and wrong.

When you check out at shop.example.com, the natural mental model is a straight line: your browser sends the card to the shop, the shop asks the bank, the bank replies. Something like this:

The naive design — the shop sits in the middle.

Clean. Except this design breaks a promise we did not know we were making.

The shop is not one server. It is a running application, with a frontend, a backend, request logs, error trackers, analytics, backups, a queue for slow work, a team of engineers who can search production data, and dozens of third-party dependencies loaded into the page by the browser. Anywhere the card touches, the card can leak.

Even the shop's honest employees are a problem. They should not be one careless log line away from a database of live card numbers.

So the real question is not how do we send the card safely. It is:

How do we let a customer enter a card on the shop's page without the shop ever holding it?

That question is the whole article.

02 — The first misconception

HTTPS looks like enough. It isn't.

HTTPS protects the wire. It stops at the front door.

The instinct of most engineers is to say: fine, send the card over HTTPS. That defeats someone sniffing coffee-shop Wi-Fi, and the URL bar shows the reassuring lock icon. Problem solved.

HTTPS does solve exactly what it advertises: the network path between the browser and the intended server is confidential, authenticated, and tamper-evident.

But the moment the shop's backend decrypts the request, HTTPS is done. What remains is a payload sitting in application memory that any of the following can see or accidentally record:

  • The web framework's request logger.
  • Whatever middleware runs before the payment handler — auth, rate limits, telemetry, feature flags.
  • The distributed tracer, which stringifies request bodies for debugging.
  • The error tracker, which uploads the failing request to a third-party service.
  • The message queue that a downstream worker reads from.
  • The database backup that copies rows to cold storage overnight.
  • The support tool that lets a human "replay" a customer's failed order.

None of these are careless — they exist for good reasons. But every one of them is now a place where a card number can live longer than it should.

The stronger statement: if raw card data reaches the shop's server, every system that can receive, process, persist, debug, replay, back up, or observe that request has become part of the card-security problem.

The realization is that we are asking the wrong question. Better encryption between the browser and the shop does not help, because the shop was never supposed to see the card in the first place.

The goal is not protect the card inside the shop. The goal is keep the card out of the shop entirely.

Which is a strange goal, because the customer is typing the card on the shop's page. So who is receiving it, and how did they get in?

03 — The cast

Six parties, one payment.

Before the architecture makes sense, the vocabulary has to.

Almost every confusing conversation about payments turns out to be a vocabulary problem in disguise. So, briefly, the players:

The six parties. The provider is highlighted because it does the work this article is about.

A single company frequently plays several of these roles at once. Stripe, for instance, is your PSP, but it also connects to acquiring banks and card networks on your behalf. The conceptual roles stay useful even when one API hides them all.

And two of these six parties share your browser tab: the merchant, whose page you are on, and the payment provider, whose card form is sitting inside it. Somehow both of them are in the same window, and only one of them is allowed to see the digits.

That is the trick worth understanding first.

04 — A wall you cannot see

The card field looks embedded. It belongs to somebody else.

The browser draws an invisible line through the checkout page.

Load a modern checkout and you will see a form: card number, expiry, CVC. Type into the card field and it feels like any other input on the shop's page. Border, cursor, placeholder text — all styled by the shop.

Peek at the DOM in your browser's dev tools and something odd shows up. The input is not really an input. It is an <iframe>, and the document inside that iframe is served from a completely different domain — something like https://js.stripe.com/, not https://shop.example.com/.

The shop styled the outer container. The payment provider rendered the actual field.

Two documents. One browser tab. The wall is invisible but strictly enforced.

The wall is called the Same-Origin Policy. The rule is short: a script running on one origin cannot read the DOM of a document served from a different origin. "Origin" here means the exact combination of scheme, host, and port. shop.example.com and js.stripe.com are different origins. The wall is between them.

So even though the shop's JavaScript is running on the same page as the card field, it cannot read the value of that input. It cannot copy the DOM. It cannot attach a listener to the field's keydown events. Every one of those operations is refused by the browser itself, not by a polite request.

Concept · Hosted fields

The pattern of embedding a provider-owned form inside a merchant page via a cross-origin iframe has an industry name: hosted fields. Stripe Elements, Braintree Hosted Fields, and Adyen Web Components are all implementations of the same idea.

The merchant loads the provider's small SDK, calls something like elements.create('card'), and points it at an empty <div> on the page. The SDK inserts an iframe into that div. Everything sensitive lives inside the iframe.

An iframe is a containment boundary, not a magic shield. A malicious script running on the shop's page cannot read the iframe's inputs, but it can hide the iframe, cover it with a fake overlay, or redirect the entire checkout. We will return to this in chapter 11.

The wall settles one question and creates another. The shop cannot read the card. Good. But the shop still needs to know whether the field is even filled in, so the Pay button can enable. How does the shop learn that much without learning too much?

05 — Talking through the wall

The iframe sends postcards, not the card.

Enough information to run a checkout. Nothing more.

The browser gives cross-origin windows a narrow way to talk: window.postMessage(). It is a message-passing API, not a shared-memory API. One window can send a message; the other window can listen for it and choose what to do.

Hosted fields use postMessage to tell the shop only the things the shop needs to know:

  • The card field is complete. — so the Pay button can enable.
  • It's a Visa. — so the shop can show the correct brand logo.
  • The expiry is invalid. — so the shop can show an inline error.

None of these are the card number. None of them are the CVC. They are metadata about a value the shop is not allowed to see.

Merchant page — parent window
// Listen for messages from the payment provider iframe.
window.addEventListener('message', (event) => {

  // Rule 1: check the sender's origin, exactly.
  if (event.origin !== 'https://js.stripe.com') return;

  // Rule 2: validate the shape of what you received.
  const msg = validatePaymentFieldMessage(event.data);
  if (!msg) return;

  // Rule 3: act on only the fields you expect.
  payButton.disabled = !msg.complete;
  brandLogo.src = brandFor(msg.brand);
});

// Never: event.origin.endsWith('stripe.com')
//        — evil-stripe.com passes that check.

The three rules — exact origin, validated shape, expected fields — are the difference between a safe listener and a hole. The failure mode is easy to picture: an attacker embeds their own iframe from evil-stripe.com, sends a message that looks legitimate, and the shop's page happily believes them.

So the shop now knows the customer has typed something Visa-shaped and complete. The Pay button lights up. The customer clicks it. And at that moment, the iframe does the one thing the shop cannot do: it takes the card, and sends it directly to the payment provider over TLS. The shop's servers are not on that path at all.

Same-origin policy decides who receives the card. TLS protects the path to that receiver. Both are required.

Seconds later, the iframe sends the shop one more message. This one is the point of everything so far.

06 — The receipt

What comes back is a claim ticket for something you never touched.

Tokenization, and the smallest object the merchant needs.

The card is now inside the payment provider. In exchange, the provider hands the merchant an opaque identifier — a token, sometimes called a payment method ID. It looks like this:

What the provider returns to the browser{
  "payment_method_id": "pm_7Hk9x2Ktm3aVzQ4pL",
  "card": {
    "brand":     "visa",
    "last4":     "9012",
    "exp_month": 12,
    "exp_year":  2030
  }
}

// The full PAN is not here.
// The CVC is not here, and is not stored.

The merchant page receives this JSON, hands it to the merchant's own backend along with the order details, and the whole thing gets treated like a coat-check ticket. The shop never had the coat; it only has the number on the little paper stub. Handing the stub to the provider says charge whatever this represents.

Merchant frontend → merchant backendPOST /api/orders/checkout
{
  "order_id":         "ord_1042",
  "amount":           499900,
  "currency":         "INR",
  "payment_method_id": "pm_7Hk9x2Ktm3aVzQ4pL"
}

Notice what is not in that payload. No PAN. No CVC. No expiry beyond what the merchant needs to display. From this point on, every merchant system — the order service, the fulfillment queue, the finance database, the analytics warehouse, the support console, the nightly backup — can traffic in tokens and never once see a card number.

The token has three properties that make it work:

  1. It is opaque. Nothing about pm_7Hk9x2Ktm3aVzQ4pL reveals the card behind it. You cannot decode it.
  2. It is scoped. The token is only usable inside the account that created it, from the API credentials that own it. Steal the token, and without the account's server-side secret key, you have a piece of string.
  3. It is revocable. The provider can invalidate a token at any time without changing the underlying card.
The shift in what the merchant stores: instead of a secret (the card), the merchant stores a pointer (the token). The provider decides what the pointer resolves to, who is allowed to resolve it, and for what.

So the shop is safe. The token is useless without the shop's server-side credential, and the card itself never left the provider. The natural next question is: fine, the provider has the card now. Where does the provider actually keep it, and who at the provider gets to see it?

07 — Inside the vault

Even inside the provider, the card is kept in the smallest possible room.

Not every service at the payment company is allowed to see a card number.

Imagine designing the provider's own architecture. The card has arrived. You need to store it somewhere so future charges on this payment method can succeed. The naive answer is: put it in a database, encrypt the column, done.

The mature answer is stranger, and much more careful. It has three ideas stacked on top of each other.

Idea 1 — Separate the record from the key.

The encrypted card record sits in one system. The key that decrypts it sits in another. Stealing the database gets you ciphertext. Stealing the key management system gets you keys with nothing to decrypt. You need both, and they are protected by different controls.

Idea 2 — Wrap keys with keys.

The card is not encrypted directly with a master key. It is encrypted with a per-record or per-tenant data encryption key (DEK). That DEK is itself encrypted — "wrapped" — under a longer-lived key encryption key (KEK). The KEK never leaves a hardened boundary. Rotating a DEK is a small operation. Rotating a KEK is a rare, well-audited one. This layered structure is called envelope encryption.

Idea 3 — Put the root key inside hardware that will not release it.

The KEK lives inside a Hardware Security Module (HSM) — a cryptographic appliance designed so that keys can be used inside it but not extracted. You can ask the HSM to unwrap a DEK for you; you cannot ask it to hand over the KEK itself. FIPS 140-3 is the widely used standard that describes what "hardened" means here.

Envelope encryption. Each layer protects the one above it.

Reading the card back is a small ceremony. A tightly restricted service asks the HSM to unwrap the DEK; the DEK is used to decrypt the PAN record; the plaintext PAN is used to build the outbound authorization message; and then everything is discarded. No other service — not analytics, not the support tool, not the fraud engine, not the general billing pipeline — is on that path at all. Network segmentation, workload identity, and explicit authorization all say no to anyone else who asks.

Concept · HSM

A Hardware Security Module is a purpose-built cryptographic appliance. It performs signing, decryption, and key wrapping inside a sealed enclosure, enforces role-based authentication for those operations, and responds to physical tampering by destroying its keys.

An HSM is not a substitute for good architecture. It is a strong last resort for the specific goal of making key extraction extremely difficult even when the surrounding software fails.

There is one piece of card data even this vault refuses to keep. Not because it cannot — because the rules forbid it. And this is where payment security is at its strictest.

08 — The rule that never bends

The CVC is used once, and then it is gone.

Some data cannot be stored, no matter how well you would store it.

The three digits on the back of the card — the CVC, or CVV, or CID, depending on the network — exist to prove that whoever is trying to charge the card has physical access to it. That guarantee is worth exactly nothing if the CVC is written down after the first use.

So the rule is short and absolute: the CVC must not be retained after the transaction is authorized. Not encrypted. Not tokenized. Not "held briefly in Redis just in case the customer wants to retry." The moment the bank replies to the authorization request, the CVC is discarded. This rule is PCI DSS at its most inflexible.

"Encrypt the CVC" is not a valid compromise. The point is not to protect the CVC from a database breach. The point is that a stored CVC allows silent replay of a full card-present-style credential without the cardholder present. Its value comes precisely from not existing anywhere between transactions.

This creates an obvious question: if the CVC is thrown away, how do saved cards work? How does a subscription service charge you next month without asking you to re-enter the CVC?

The answer is that saved cards work with weaker guarantees. A future charge on a saved card uses the stored PAN (or a network token, which we will meet in the next chapter), the stored expiry, and the merchant's demonstration that this is a legitimate recurring or merchant-initiated transaction. It cannot present a fresh CVC because there isn't one. The card networks accommodate this with special transaction indicators — this is a recurring charge, not a fresh purchase — and the issuer takes that into account when deciding whether to approve.

The PAN, meanwhile, may live longer. Inside the vault, protected by the layers we just built. Ready to be resolved when the next charge comes in.

But using that PAN turns out to be more interesting than storing it. Because the card networks would prefer, when possible, that even the PAN never leave the vault. And that is where a second kind of token appears.

09 — Two kinds of tokens

The word "token" hides two very different things.

A gateway's private reference is not a card network's substitute credential.

Everything so far has used one word — token — for one thing: the opaque identifier the merchant gets back from the payment provider. That is a PSP token, or gateway token. It is a private handle inside a single company's systems.

There is a second animal with the same name, issued by the card networks themselves. It is called a network token, and it is defined by an EMVCo standard called EMV Payment Tokenisation. Visa, Mastercard, and Amex all operate their own token services under it.

The point of a network token is that the PAN itself never needs to travel to the merchant, the acquirer, or the network for a transaction. The merchant holds a network token. The token flows through the payment chain. Only the issuer's tokenization service can map that token back to the underlying account. The real card number stays behind a very different wall.

PSP tokenNetwork token
Issued by The payment provider (Stripe, Adyen, Braintree, …) The card network (Visa TS, Mastercard MDES, …)
Scope Usable inside that provider's account and API Usable across the card network with domain restrictions
Replaces A reference to a stored card in the provider's vault The PAN itself, in messages sent along the payment rails
Transaction proof Provider's server-side API credentials A per-transaction cryptogram generated with the token
If it leaks Useless without the provider's secret key Useless outside its bound domain, and requires a valid cryptogram
Lifecycle Provider updates when card is reissued (if the network supports it) Network keeps the token stable when the card is reissued — the number under it changes, the token does not

Network tokens have a property that PSP tokens do not: the token itself is bound to a domain of use. A token issued for card-on-file at a specific merchant will be rejected if it shows up somewhere else. A token bound to a specific device will not work from a different device. And each transaction needs a fresh cryptogram, which prevents replay.

A stolen PSP token gets you nothing without the provider's server-side credential. A stolen network token gets you nothing without its bound domain and a valid cryptogram. Both are strong. They just draw the boundary in different places.

So the token that leaves the vault, on its way to the bank, may not be a card number at all. It may be a card-network-issued substitute with a cryptographic proof attached. But either way, the message now has to travel out of the payment provider, across the financial rails, and into the bank that issued the card. Which is a longer journey than most people realize.

10 — The long path to the bank

The message leaves the provider and enters an older world.

Modern JSON goes in. A 1980s message format comes out. It works.

The merchant's backend has called the payment provider with a modern REST request: charge this token for this amount. Inside the provider, the token has been resolved into either a PAN or a network token with a cryptogram, wrapped for outbound transmission, and handed to the connector that speaks to acquiring banks.

From here the message is no longer JSON. It is ISO 8583, or a network-specific dialect derived from it — a binary message format designed decades ago for card networks, still used by most of them. It contains a message type, a bitmap indicating which fields are present, and the fields themselves: account credential, amount, currency, merchant category, terminal type, response code, and dozens more.

Down the chain of authorization. Back up the chain, a single approved/declined bit.

The issuer — the bank that gave you the card — is the only party allowed to decide whether the charge succeeds. It looks at whatever combination of these it can:

  • Is the account active? Not blocked, not expired, not reported stolen.
  • Are the funds or credit available?
  • Does the transaction look normal? Right country, right merchant category, right velocity.
  • Was the cardholder authenticated? Did a 3-D Secure challenge succeed?

The answer is a single response code that flows back up the same chain: acquirer → provider → merchant → your screen. Approved. Or declined, insufficient funds. Or declined, requires authentication.

Concept · 3-D Secure

3-D Secure (EMV 3DS) is an authentication protocol layered on top of card payments, primarily for card-not-present transactions. When a payment looks risky, the issuer can require the cardholder to authenticate — a bank-app tap, a biometric prompt, an OTP.

3DS is not tokenization and is not encryption. It is a way for the issuer to say prove it's really you without asking the merchant to handle a password. When it succeeds, a piece of evidence rides along with the authorization message, and the liability for fraud shifts to the issuer.

And that, in the merchant's world, is the end of the payment story. A row in the orders table flips from pending to paid. The customer sees a confirmation page. The shop dispatches the headphones.

Except we are not done. We have protected the vault. We have protected the wire. We have protected the message. There are three more places where a real payment system can still fail — and each of them has ended in a headline before.

11 — The page is still dangerous

The iframe was not enough. It was never going to be.

An attacker cannot read the card field. They can replace it.

Here is the failure mode. A merchant's checkout page loads eleven third-party scripts: an analytics library, a tag manager, a chat widget, a session replay tool, three A/B testing scripts, and four things nobody remembers adding. One of them — often not the merchant's own JavaScript, but a dependency of a dependency of a dependency — is compromised.

The compromised script cannot read the iframe. Same-origin policy still holds. But it can do this: draw a pixel-perfect fake card form directly over the real iframe, capture the keystrokes into itself, and quietly send them to an attacker's server. To the customer, everything looks normal. The Pay button even works — the malicious script forwards the card to the real provider after it has copied it.

This is called e-skimming, or a Magecart attack after the group that industrialized it. The vault we built is untouched. The wire is untouched. The card just never made it to the vault, because it was intercepted before it left the browser.

The mental correction: hosted fields reduce the shop's exposure to the card, but they do not remove the shop's responsibility for the checkout page. The page is now the smallest and most valuable attack surface.

PCI DSS v4 responded to this with two requirements aimed squarely at the payment page: 6.4.3 (know which scripts run on the payment page, authorize each one, and confirm their integrity) and 11.6.1 (detect unauthorized changes to headers and script content). Together they push toward a small idea with a large operational tail: you must know, at every moment, exactly what is running on your checkout page.

Concretely, that looks like:

  • An inventory of every script the payment page loads, refreshed continuously.
  • Content Security Policy headers that reject scripts from anywhere else.
  • Subresource Integrity hashes for pinned dependencies, where operationally feasible.
  • A monitor that alerts when a script's content or a security header changes without a release.
  • A ruthless minimum: no marketing tag or analytics library on the checkout page by default.

The last one is the hardest culturally and the biggest lever technically. A checkout page with three scripts is auditable. A checkout page with eleven is a question of when.

So the page is defended. So is the vault. What about the space between them?

12 — No trust inside either

The provider's own network is not automatically friendly.

A perfect vault does not help if the log line beside it is a card number.

Inside the payment provider, the vault does one job: hold the card. Everything else — routing, risk scoring, dispute handling, fee calculation, statements, dashboards — is done by dozens of other services. None of them need the card. Almost all of them touch payment data.

The rule inside the provider is roughly: no implicit trust and no accidental copies.

No implicit trust means every internal call is authenticated. A service is not trusted because it is on the same VPC. Mutual TLS establishes both sides of every connection; workload identity confirms which specific service is calling; explicit authorization policy decides whether that service is allowed to invoke this specific operation. Can this workload de-tokenize a PAN? is a policy question, not a network question.

No accidental copies means observability is the leak path. This is subtle and worth showing:

Two log lines. One is a disaster.// Dangerous — the whole request body ends up in logs.
logger.info("payment request = {}", requestBody);

// Better — only allow-listed fields.
logger.info(
  "payment attempt order={} pm={} amount={} status={}",
  orderId, paymentMethodId, amount, status
);

// Best — never depend on developers remembering.
// Use secret-aware types, structural allow-lists,
// central redaction, restricted debug access,
// and data-loss-prevention scanners as defense in depth.

A payment platform that gets this right treats sensitive values the way a well-designed type system treats units. A Pan type has no default toString(). A Cvc type refuses to be serialized. A Card object cannot enter the general-purpose structured logger. The redaction regex that scans for card-shaped digit sequences in log pipelines exists — but it is the safety net beneath the design, not the design itself.

Which services should ever see a full PAN? Almost none of them.

Every layer we have built so far protects the secret. There is one more class of failure that a perfect secret cannot prevent: charging you twice.

13 — A different kind of failure

A secure double charge is still a broken payment.

The card was safe. The customer was charged twice. Both statements are true.

Consider a specific bad moment. The merchant's backend calls the payment provider. The provider forwards the authorization. The issuer approves. And then, before the response reaches the merchant, a load balancer times out. The merchant sees a network error.

The merchant does not know whether the charge succeeded. Retrying might create a second charge. Not retrying might mean the customer paid and the shop shipped nothing.

None of the machinery we have built so far solves this problem. Envelope encryption does not know whether a request completed. TLS does not distinguish a lost response from a failed one. This is a correctness problem, not a security problem, and it needs its own tools.

An idempotency key prevents a duplicate operation. A token prevents PAN exposure. They are unrelated tools for unrelated failures.

The primary tool is the idempotency key: a client-generated unique identifier attached to each write. The rule the provider follows is simple. The first time the provider sees a given key, it performs the operation, records the outcome, and returns the result. The second, third, and hundredth time it sees the same key, it does not perform the operation again — it returns the stored result of the first attempt.

A safe retryPOST /v1/payment_intents/pi_xyz/confirm
Idempotency-Key: "ord_1042-confirm-a4f2"

// First attempt: charge succeeds, response is lost in transit.
// Second attempt: same key, provider returns the same result.
//                 No second charge.

// A conflicting reuse (same key, different amount) is refused,
// not silently accepted.

Sitting under the idempotency key is a small state machine for the payment intent itself: created → requires_action → processing → succeeded | failed → reconciled. Each terminal state is durable and cannot be moved out of. When the merchant is unsure, it does not guess — it queries the provider or reconciles from the provider's webhook. The state machine is the single source of truth about what happened; the network timeout is just noise.

Card data security and financial correctness travel together but do not overlap. Hosted fields, tokenization, TLS, vault isolation, and log discipline protect the secret. Idempotency keys, durable state, and reconciliation protect the outcome. A serious payment system needs both, and neither implies the other.

That is every part. Time to assemble them.

14 — The complete picture

From keystroke to bank response, in one walk.

This is the mental model. Everything above is why each piece exists.

A customer opens shop.example.com/checkout. Order total: ₹4,999. The merchant's page loads the payment provider's SDK. The SDK inserts an iframe from js.stripe.com into a container on the page. Card, expiry, CVC are painted, but they live inside the iframe's document — a different origin from the shop.

The customer types the digits. The iframe reports back through postMessage: Visa, complete, no errors. The Pay button lights up. The customer clicks it.

The iframe opens a TLS connection to the provider's tokenization endpoint, sends the card details, and receives back a payment method ID: pm_7Hk9x2Ktm3aVzQ4pL, along with the brand and last four digits. The shop's servers were nowhere on that path.

The merchant's frontend hands the payment method ID, the order ID, the amount, and the currency to the merchant's backend. The backend recalculates the amount from the order — because a client-provided total is not a source of truth — and calls the payment provider's API with a server-side secret key and a fresh idempotency key.

Inside the provider, an edge service authenticates the API credential, checks rate limits, and passes the request to the orchestrator. A risk service scores it. If the score demands it, a 3-D Secure challenge is invoked and the customer authenticates with their bank. The vault is asked to resolve the token: it retrieves the encrypted PAN record, asks the HSM to unwrap the DEK, decrypts the PAN into memory just long enough to build the outbound message, and hands it to the acquirer connector.

The acquirer connector translates the request into ISO 8583 and sends it to an acquiring bank. The acquirer routes to the card network based on the card's BIN. The card network delivers the message to the issuer. The issuer looks at account status, funds, risk signals, and the 3DS evidence, and decides approved. The response climbs back up: issuer → network → acquirer → provider. The provider marks the payment intent succeeded, sends a webhook to the merchant, and returns the success response.

The merchant's backend updates the order to paid. The frontend shows a confirmation page. The customer sees Payment successful.

Every one of these steps had a reason. Here are the truths that survive the walk:

  1. HTTPS protects data on the wire; it does not keep data out of the application that decrypted it.
  2. Hosted fields put the sensitive card inputs inside a provider-owned iframe from a different origin.
  3. The browser's Same-Origin Policy prevents ordinary merchant scripts from reading that iframe's DOM.
  4. Cross-origin communication uses postMessage, and only carries non-sensitive status.
  5. The card travels from the iframe directly to the provider over TLS, bypassing the merchant backend.
  6. The merchant receives a token — a payment method ID — not the PAN.
  7. A token is opaque, scoped to the account, and useless without the server-side API credential.
  8. Inside the vault, envelope encryption separates the encrypted record, the data key, and the root key.
  9. The root key lives inside an HSM, which lets keys be used but not extracted.
  10. The CVC is used once and then discarded — it must not be retained after authorization.
  11. A network token replaces the PAN in the payment message itself, bound to a domain, proven by a cryptogram.
  12. The authorization message becomes ISO 8583 as it enters the card networks, and the issuer decides.
  13. The iframe blocks DOM reading but not overlays; the checkout page needs script inventory and integrity monitoring.
  14. Inside the provider, mTLS, workload identity, secret-aware types, and central redaction keep secrets out of logs and traces.
  15. Idempotency keys and a durable payment state machine protect the customer from being charged twice by a lost response.
  16. Every layer minimizes who can see the credential, how long they can see it, and what they can do with it.
The shop embeds a card field it does not own. The browser sends the card directly to the provider. The provider returns a reference. Every other system uses that reference, and only the smallest, most tightly controlled boundary inside the provider is ever allowed to resolve it.

You still typed your card. The shop still sold you headphones. Between those two events, sixteen digits were seen by exactly the systems that had to see them — and by none of the ones that didn't.

That was the whole design.
