    How we send
    one live moment
    to millions of screens.

    After years of anticipation, Conor McGregor’s return lasted just 70 seconds. He launched a kick, landed awkwardly on his right knee, and his fight against Max Holloway ended almost before it had begun. It was a deeply disappointing moment. But within seconds, millions of us watched it unfold on phones, laptops, and televisions around the world. So how did one moment inside an arena in Las Vegas travel across the world and reach our screens almost instantly? Let us look behind that single Watch Live button.

    00 · Prologue

    The button that hides a system.

    You tap Watch Live. The video starts.

    Maybe it is sharp 1080p. Maybe your internet slows down and the picture becomes blurry for a few seconds. Then the quality quietly improves again.

    You never choose a server. You never download a file. You never tell the application that your WiFi just became slower.

    The stream simply keeps playing.

    But behind that small Watch Live button is a system solving several difficult problems at the same time:

    • How does one camera feed reach millions of people?
    • How can the same event work on both fast broadband and unstable mobile internet?
    • How can the video change quality without restarting?
    • And why are you still seeing the action a few seconds after it actually happened?

    Let us build the system one problem at a time.

    01 · The illusion

    It feels like a direct connection.

    The camera captures. The frame travels. Your screen displays.

    When you watch a live event, it feels as though your phone is connected directly to the camera. Something like this:

    The naive design: direct connection.

    Simple. Except this design breaks almost immediately.

    A major event may have millions of viewers. If every device received its own copy directly from the source, the source would need to maintain millions of connections and repeatedly send the same video bytes across the internet.

    Even worse, every viewer has a different network. One person may have a 500 Mbps broadband connection. Another may be watching on unstable mobile data inside a moving train. A single high quality video feed cannot work well for both.

    And there is another problem. A live event does not have an ending yet. When you open a movie, the complete file already exists. A live stream is being created while people are watching it.

    So our real goal is not simply:

    Send video from a camera to a screen.

    It is this:

    Continuously turn one live production feed into millions of reliable viewing experiences across different devices, locations, and network conditions.
    02 · The first problem

    One stream, many internet speeds.

    You cannot send the same version to everyone.

    Suppose the platform sends a single 1080p feed at 5 Mbps to every viewer. Someone on 2 Mbps cannot download the video as quickly as it is being played. Every second, the player falls further behind. Eventually the buffer empties. The video freezes.

    Send everyone a smaller video instead? Now viewers with fast internet receive poor quality even though their connection could support something much better.

    We need several versions of the same live event.

    The fix: create a bitrate ladder

    The production feed enters a set of video encoders that continuously create multiple versions of the same stream. This process is called transcoding, and each output is called a rendition.

    The bitrate ladder: one input, many parallel outputs.

    A rendition is not defined only by resolution. It also has a bitrate, codec, frame rate, and other encoding settings.

    Concept note · Codec

    A codec is the algorithm used to compress raw video into fewer bytes for transmission and decode those bytes back into video on the viewer's device. H.264, HEVC, and AV1 are different codecs.

    Newer codecs such as HEVC and AV1 can deliver similar visual quality at roughly 30 to 50 percent lower bitrate, but they require more processing power and are not supported by every device.

    Now we have a version for almost every viewer. But we have created a new problem. Suppose a viewer starts in 1080p. Thirty seconds later, their WiFi becomes slow. How do we move them to 480p without restarting the video?

    We cannot treat the stream as one enormous, continuous file. We need to break it apart.

    03 · Segments

    Stop thinking of video as one long file.

    Cut the stream into small, interchangeable pieces.

    Instead of producing one continuous video file, the system divides each rendition into a sequence of small media segments. Each column below represents the same moment on the timeline, encoded at different qualities.

    Aligned segments across renditions. Segment 103 at any quality contains the same slice of time.

    That alignment is not accidental. Video is compressed by describing most frames as differences from earlier frames.

    In simple terms: the stream is not cut at random points. Each segment begins with a complete, independently decodable keyframe. This ensures that when the next segment starts, or the player switches to another quality, it does not depend on frames from the previous segment.

    To switch qualities cleanly, the player has to begin decoding a rendition at a frame that stands on its own: an IDR keyframe. So every encoder in the ladder is instructed to place a keyframe at exactly the same points on the timeline, and segments are cut on those keyframes.

    Now watch what happens when the network changes

    The alignment lets the player finish one segment and request the next segment at a different quality, with no restart.

    Adaptive bitrate in action: the player picks a quality per segment based on network health.

    The viewer may notice that the picture became softer, but playback continues. This is the foundation of adaptive bitrate streaming. The stream is no longer one endless file. It is a timeline made from many small, interchangeable pieces.

    Why not make the pieces extremely small?

    Smaller segments allow the player to react more quickly. If each segment lasts six seconds, the player may need to wait longer before changing quality. Two second segments adapt sooner. But smaller segments also create more requests, more metadata, and reduce compression efficiency, because keyframes are expensive and shorter segments mean more of them.

    Segment size is a tradeoff. Large segments are efficient but less responsive. Small segments react faster but increase overhead.

    04 · The manifest

    A small file that describes the stream.

    Before any video moves, the player asks for a map.

    When you press Watch Live, the application does not immediately download video. It first requests a small text document called a manifest or playlist. Conceptually, it might say:

    stream.m3u8
    Available qualities
    1080p→5 MbpsHIGH
    720p→2.5 MbpsMID
    480p→1 MbpsLOW

    Latest available segments
    101102103104 ●
    Concept note · HLS, DASH and CMAF

    HLS, or HTTP Live Streaming, is Apple’s streaming protocol. It uses a playlist to tell the player which video qualities and segments are available.

    MPEG DASH, or Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP, does the same job using a different manifest format. HLS usually uses .m3u8, while DASH uses .mpd.

    The actual video segments can use CMAF, or Common Media Application Format, allowing both HLS and DASH to point to the same video files.

    The player reads the manifest, chooses a quality, and downloads the matching segments. As the event continues, new segments enter the live playlist and older ones move out of its current window.

    At this point our architecture looks like this:

    The full delivery pipeline, from source to screen.

    This works for one viewer. Perhaps even a few thousand viewers. Then the event becomes popular. Five million people request the same new segment within a few seconds. And our origin server melts.

    05 · The scale problem

    Millions of viewers want the same bytes.

    Put copies close to the people who want them.

    Imagine that segment 8,421 contains the winning goal. Every viewer watching near the live edge needs that segment. Without another layer, every request travels back to the origin. Its network capacity becomes the bottleneck.

    The problem is unusual because the system is doing enormous work while serving highly repetitive data. Millions of people are not requesting millions of different videos. They are requesting the same few newly created segments. That repetition gives us the solution.

    The fix: a Content Delivery Network

    A CDN operates servers in many geographic locations, called edge locations or points of presence. When a viewer requests a segment, the request goes to a nearby CDN edge rather than directly to the origin.

    One origin, many edges, millions of viewers. Most bytes are served from the nearest edge cache.

    Suppose the first viewer near Mumbai requests segment 8,421. The edge may not have it yet, which creates a cache miss. The edge retrieves the segment from the origin and stores a temporary copy. The next thousands of nearby viewers request the same segment. Now the edge already has it. Those are cache hits.

    The CDN absorbs most of the video traffic. The origin remains the source of truth for newly produced segments, but it no longer needs to deliver every byte directly to every viewer.

    Live streaming has a particularly useful form of locality: most viewers are watching approximately the same moment. When a new segment appears, many people request it within a short time window. That makes newly produced segments extremely valuable to cache.

    The system now scales. But there is still a difficult question. Who decides whether the next segment should be 1080p, 720p, or 480p? Not the encoder. Not the CDN. The decision is made on the viewer's device.

    06 · The player

    The player is the control system.

    It is doing much more than displaying video.

    A modern video player is not a passive screen. It continuously observes playback and makes decisions. It watches:

    • how quickly recent segments downloaded;
    • how much playable video is already buffered;
    • whether the network appears stable;
    • which qualities the device can decode.

    Before requesting the next segment, the player asks:

    Which quality gives me the best picture without risking a stall?

    Suppose the viewer's network currently supports about 8 Mbps. The player may choose 1080p. Then the viewer enters an elevator. Available bandwidth drops. The latest segment takes longer to download. The buffer begins shrinking.

    The player does not wait for the video to freeze. It requests a smaller version of the next segment. The picture becomes blurry, but the video keeps moving. A few seconds of lower quality is better than a frozen screen. When the connection improves and the buffer becomes healthy, the player climbs back to a higher quality.

    This process is called Adaptive Bitrate, or ABR, selection.

    Why does the player keep a buffer?

    The player usually downloads slightly ahead of what you are currently watching. That downloaded video becomes a safety reserve.

    If the network stalls for a second, the buffer keeps playback smooth. It is a safety reserve.

    Without a buffer, every small network delay would immediately freeze the stream. But buffering creates a tradeoff: a larger buffer improves stability, a smaller buffer keeps the viewer closer to the actual event.

    07 · Failure

    Live events do not have a retry button.

    Reliability has to begin at the source.

    A movie can be processed again tomorrow. A live final cannot. If the only network connection from the stadium fails during the winning moment, no downstream CDN can recreate the missing video.

    Large platforms may use multiple independent contribution paths from the venue, and duplicate the processing pipeline across regions. If one path loses packets or fails, the other continues carrying the feed.

    Two independent contribution paths converge at ingest. If one fails, the other carries the feed.

    Large platforms may process the same live feed through two independent pipelines, often in separate regions. One pipeline serves the stream while the other remains ready to take over.

    However, the backup pipeline must produce the same timeline as the primary one. Both pipelines use the same source timestamps, keyframe positions, segment duration, and encoding settings, so they cut the stream at exactly the same moments.

    For example, if Region A fails after segment 102, the system can serve segment 103 from Region B. Because both pipelines created that segment for the same point in time, playback can continue without repeating or skipping part of the event.

    The goal is not simply to keep another encoder running. The backup output must be a compatible continuation of the primary stream.

    08 · Latency

    Where the delay comes from.

    A fighter lands a punch. The crowd inside the arena erupts instantly. I see the same moment several seconds later.

    That delay did not come from one slow server. It accumulated across the entire pipeline.

    Each stage adds a little time. Typical HLS or DASH end to end delay: 10 to 45 seconds.

    Together, these delays create the gap between what is happening inside the arena and what the viewer sees.

    One major source is segment creation. Suppose the packager creates six second segments. In normal streaming, it may wait until all six seconds have been encoded before publishing the segment. Only then can the player request it from the CDN. The player may also keep several more seconds buffered for safety.

    The low latency idea

    Where this happens

    Encoder → Packager → Origin → CDN → Player. The encoder produces compressed video frames. The packager groups those frames into segments and smaller parts. The origin stores or exposes them, the CDN delivers them, and the player makes the HTTP requests.

    Low latency streaming does not remove the six second segment. Instead, it exposes smaller pieces while that segment is still being created.

    Normal streaming may wait until the complete six second segment is ready.

    The packager divides that same segment into smaller pieces called parts in Low Latency HLS or chunks in CMAF. If a six second segment contains four equal pieces, each piece represents 1.5 seconds of video.

    The same segment exposed as four 1.5 second pieces, each published as soon as it is ready.

    As soon as Part 1 is ready, the player can download and begin decoding it. It does not have to wait for Parts 2, 3, and 4 or for the full six second segment to finish.

    How the player discovers a new part

    The player reads the live playlist to learn which parts are available. Repeatedly downloading the playlist would create wasteful polling.

    With a blocking playlist reload, the player requests a future playlist update from the CDN. The CDN or origin keeps that HTTP request open until the next part becomes available, then returns the updated playlist immediately. The player can then request the newly listed part.

    Related technique · Progressive chunked delivery

    Another approach lets the player request a segment before the complete file exists. The server starts the HTTP response with the bytes already available and continues sending more bytes through the same open response as they are produced.

    The player can begin decoding the first bytes while the end of the segment is still being created. The key idea is the same: do not wait for the complete segment before delivery begins.

    Normal streaming waits for the complete segment. Low latency streaming begins delivering smaller pieces as soon as they are ready. This reduces delay while preserving HTTP delivery and CDN caching.

    When even that is too slow

    Some products, including live auctions, interactive game shows, sports betting, and remote camera control, cannot tolerate even a few seconds of delay. For these, platforms reach for WebRTC, which can deliver latency below one second by giving up on segmented HTTP delivery entirely and using a persistent peer connection instead. The tradeoff is real: WebRTC does not cache the way HTTP does, so scaling it to millions of viewers requires specialized fanout infrastructure that most CDNs do not natively provide.

    Why not reduce latency to almost zero for everyone?

    Because lower latency removes safety margin. A player with very little buffered video has less protection from network variation. Live streaming balances two competing goals:

    More buffering
    • More stable playback
    • Survives network hiccups
    • Higher end to end delay
    Less buffering
    • Lower delay, closer to live
    • Feels real time
    • Greater risk of stalls

    A sports broadcast may tolerate five seconds of delay to remain stable for millions of viewers. A live auction may accept a smaller audience in exchange for interaction that is nearly real time. The correct architecture depends on the product.

    09 · The rest of the payload

    A live stream carries more than video.

    Content protection. Paid streams encrypt their media segments. Before playback begins, the player requests a license from a DRM system such as Widevine, FairPlay, or PlayReady. CMAF Common Encryption allows the same encrypted segments to work across these systems while only the license exchange changes.

    Ads. With server side ad insertion, the platform places ad segments directly into each viewer’s manifest. To the player, the advertisement appears as part of the same continuous stream, so playback and adaptive quality switching continue normally.

    Timed metadata. Scores, subtitles, betting odds, and interactive overlays are attached to specific points on the video timeline. The player reads this metadata during playback, keeping every onscreen update synchronized with the correct moment.

    10 · Hyperscale

    Every simple box becomes a distributed system.

    The origin understands live media. Viewers may request a new segment just before it is ready. Instead of returning repeated 404 errors and causing retries, an origin that understands media can briefly hold the request and respond as soon as the segment is published.

    One CDN may not be enough. Large platforms use multiple CDN providers because performance can differ by country, network, or internet provider. Playback data from real devices helps route viewers toward the healthiest CDN.

    Video traffic is not constant. A quiet interview requires less bitrate than a stadium scene with rapid movement. Because traffic can rise suddenly as visual complexity changes, delivery systems reserve capacity using expected bitrate rather than relying only on current usage.

    11 · Replay

    Replaying the entire journey.

    You open the application and tap Watch Live. What actually happens?

    The event is captured at the venue. A high quality production feed travels through redundant contribution paths into the streaming platform. The encoder continuously creates several renditions, all aligned at keyframes. The packager wraps them into CMAF segments, encrypts them under Common Encryption, and publishes manifests describing what is available. The latest segments arrive at the live origin.

    CDN edge servers pull those segments on demand and serve cached copies to nearby viewers. Your player downloads the manifest, exchanges a DRM license, selects an initial quality, buffers a few seconds of video, and begins playback.

    For every new segment, the player measures how the session is behaving. If the network is fast and the buffer is healthy, it may request a higher quality rendition. If the network slows and the buffer begins shrinking, it requests a smaller segment. Timed metadata rides along, keeping onscreen scores and overlays in sync with the video frame they belong to.

    Meanwhile, the system continues producing new video. The manifest moves forward. The CDN spreads new segments around the world. The player stays close to the live edge while keeping enough buffered video to survive small network disruptions.

    And if a contribution link, encoder, region, origin, or CDN begins failing, redundant systems attempt to take over before the viewer notices.

    All of that happens while you watch the same screen.

    FINAL ARCHITECTURE

    The full pipeline.

    From a moment at the venue to a screen in your hand.

    Live streaming is not one giant video travelling from a camera to millions of screens. It is a continuously moving pipeline.

    The encoder creates choices. Segments aligned at keyframes make those choices switchable. The manifest describes them. Encryption protects them. The CDN distributes them. The player decides which one is safe to request. The buffer hides network instability. Low latency techniques reduce the distance from reality. Timed metadata keeps the rest of the experience in sync. And redundancy keeps the event alive when parts of the system fail.

    "How do we deliver the best possible version of a moment that did not exist a few seconds ago?"
    You press one button. Behind it, a system is continuously answering.
